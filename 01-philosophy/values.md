# Spark OS Values

**Status:** Accepted — active
**Authority:** Foundational philosophy
**Approved by:** Sheldon Phillips
**Approval date:** 2026-07-21

Values define what Spark Mortgages refuses to compromise when doing so would be faster, easier, cheaper, more profitable, or more comfortable. They are commitments, not slogans, aspirations, metrics, procedures, or guarantees.

The five Values are not ranked. They operate within applicable law, regulation, professional obligations, privacy, confidentiality, contractual duties, and legitimate rights. When Values genuinely conflict, Spark must use Principles, legitimate authority, judgment, escalation, and documented decisions rather than treating any Value as mechanically supreme.

## Represent Reality Honestly

### 1. Name

Represent Reality Honestly

### 2. Commitment

Spark Mortgages represents material matters honestly according to the best evidence it can reasonably obtain, gives proportionate attention to credible contrary evidence, and does not preserve a materially misleading impression for convenience, appearance, speed, or profit.

### 3. Meaning

Spark makes consequential decisions with incomplete and changing information. This Value requires honest representation of material evidence, uncertainty, assumptions, limitations, disagreement, and mistakes.

Material matters are those that could reasonably affect a client’s understanding or choice, professional advice, risk, legal or contractual obligations, organizational judgment, or the reliability of Spark’s work.

Spark does not claim perfect knowledge or require exhaustive verification. It must, however, avoid deliberate ignorance and engage proportionately with credible evidence that could materially change what it says or decides. Privacy, confidentiality, law, and legitimate information restrictions remain binding.

### 4. Observable Behavior

Spark is living this Value when it:

- distinguishes evidence, assumption, interpretation, and uncertainty;
- corrects materially misleading impressions when they become known;
- communicates important limitations without overstating confidence;
- and reports material performance, risk, problems, and outcomes without manipulating the picture.

### 5. Trade-off

Spark accepts lost revenue, delay, embarrassment, reopened work, reduced confidence, or a less attractive narrative rather than preserve a materially misleading representation.

### 6. Failure Mode

The central violation is knowingly distorting material reality or avoiding credible contrary evidence because honesty is inconvenient.

This Value can be gamed through technically accurate statements arranged to mislead, selective facts, inflated certainty, strategic ambiguity, or confidentiality used to create a false impression. Honest mistakes, reasonable reliance, incomplete evidence, and good-faith disagreement are not automatically violations.

### 7. Derived Principles

The following are proposed downstream rules, not accepted Spark OS Principles:

- Represent material uncertainty honestly.
- Distinguish evidence from interpretation.
- Give credible contrary evidence proportionate attention.

## The Client Before the Deal

### 1. Name

The Client Before the Deal

### 2. Commitment

Spark Mortgages will sacrifice transactional benefit rather than compromise truthful and competent professional advice directed toward the client’s informed interests within lawful, available, professionally supportable, and properly scoped boundaries.

### 3. Meaning

Mortgage advice exists to help clients make informed decisions, not merely to complete transactions. Commission, volume, conversion, partner expectations, and organizational convenience must not corrupt professional advice.

The client’s informed interests are not identical to every expressed preference. Spark remains responsible for truthful explanation, competent interpretation, appropriate scope, and lawful professional boundaries. Once a client understands the material consequences and chooses among lawful and professionally supportable options, Spark should not substitute its preferred outcome merely because it would choose differently.

This Value does not require unlimited service or discovery of every theoretical alternative. Spark’s responsibility is proportionate to materiality, reasonable discoverability, competence, available information, and agreed scope.

### 4. Observable Behavior

Spark is living this Value when it:

- recommends against a transaction when proceeding would not serve the client’s informed interests;
- explains material alternatives and consequences within reasonable professional scope;
- respects informed client choices even when Spark would choose differently;
- and provides the same honesty, competence, and respect regardless of revenue potential.

### 5. Trade-off

Spark accepts lost commissions, conversions, partner goodwill, immediate revenue, or future business when protecting the client’s informed interests requires a less profitable recommendation, a referral elsewhere, or no transaction at all.

### 6. Failure Mode

The central violation is allowing transactional or organizational self-interest to corrupt advice.

This Value can be gamed by presenting the most profitable option as client-directed advice, withholding a known material alternative within reasonable scope, using “the client chose” to excuse inadequate explanation, or using “client interest” paternalistically to override an informed lawful decision.

### 7. Derived Principles

The following are proposed downstream rules, not accepted Spark OS Principles:

- Keep professional advice independent of transactional incentives.
- Support informed client choice without substituting organizational preference.
- Recommend only within lawful, competent, available, and properly scoped boundaries.

## Accountability Must Remain Clear

### 1. Name

Accountability Must Remain Clear

### 2. Commitment

Spark Mortgages keeps material accountability identifiable, proportionate to role, authority, knowledge, influence, and reasonable opportunity to act, while remaining accountable for the operating conditions it creates or approves.

### 3. Meaning

Spark works through individuals, teams, leaders, committees, vendors, systems, automation, and AI. Delegation, shared responsibility, and reasonable reliance are legitimate. Disappearance of accountability is not.

Material duties and decision rights must remain clear enough to understand who may decide, who must act, who retains oversight, and what Spark itself remains answerable for.

Accountability must also be fair. People should not be held fully responsible for systemic conditions they lacked meaningful authority, knowledge, influence, resources, or opportunity to prevent, detect, escalate, or correct. At the same time, limited control does not erase responsibility for judgment and action within one’s role.

Spark remains accountable for the systems, incentives, staffing, training, authority design, vendor choices, and working conditions it creates or approves. Automation and AI may perform work, but they do not become accountable actors.

### 4. Observable Behavior

Spark is living this Value when:

- material decisions and duties have identifiable or explicitly shared accountability;
- authority and accountability are proportionately aligned;
- failures are examined across both individual and systemic causes;
- and Spark accepts responsibility for conditions created through its own design, approval, or neglect.

### 5. Trade-off

Spark accepts slower implementation, continued oversight, deeper investigation, limits on opaque delegation, and organizational responsibility rather than allow accountability to disappear or blame to be assigned unfairly.

### 6. Failure Mode

The central violation is allowing material accountability to become ownerless, unfairly concentrated, or detached from authority.

This Value can be gamed by naming an owner who lacks authority or support, treating committee or vendor involvement as elimination of responsibility, blaming the person nearest to a failure, or allowing a successful system to continue without anyone accountable for its continuing validity.

### 7. Derived Principles

The following are proposed downstream rules, not accepted Spark OS Principles:

- Align accountability with authority and reasonable ability to act.
- Delegation must not create material ownership gaps.
- Spark remains accountable for operating conditions it creates or approves.

## Grow Without Becoming Fragile

### 1. Name

Grow Without Becoming Fragile

### 2. Commitment

Spark Mortgages will not pursue or preserve growth that predictably creates disproportionate structural fragility or materially degrades its ability to serve competently, operate reliably, learn, and meet its obligations.

### 3. Meaning

Growth is not opposed to quality. It can create specialization, experience, resources, stronger systems, and broader organizational knowledge.

Spark does not require perfect readiness before acting. Capability and growth may develop together through bounded experimentation, supervised experience, and responsible expansion.

The refusal is growth built on predictable structural degradation: chronic overload, deteriorating competence, brittle controls, disappearing learning capacity, hidden overtime, neglected systems, or exceptional effort that cannot responsibly continue.

Capability is necessary but morally neutral. It must remain directed by the other Values and external obligations. Temporary strain, urgency, difficult periods, experiments, and honest mistakes are not automatically fragility. The concern is weakness that has become structural, foreseeable, disproportionate, concealed, or accepted as the operating model.

This Value must not be used to protect comfort, low ambition, resistance to change, or endless preparation.

### 4. Observable Behavior

Spark is living this Value when it:

- considers how growth affects competence, reliability, learning, and responsible workload;
- strengthens support and operating capability as complexity increases;
- distinguishes temporary supported strain from chronic structural overload;
- and slows, redesigns, or refuses growth when material degradation cannot be responsibly contained.

### 5. Trade-off

Spark accepts lower short-term volume, reduced margins, delayed launches, declined opportunities, maintenance, supervision, or infrastructure investment rather than build success on predictable fragility. It may also accept temporary inefficiency and recoverable performance decline when risk is bounded and learning can strengthen future capability.

### 6. Failure Mode

The central violation is knowingly building or preserving growth on disproportionate structural weakness.

This Value can be gamed by treating volume or favorable metrics as proof of capability, normalizing chronic overload, concealing hidden overtime, redefining quality after decline, calling every recurring problem temporary, or using “quality” and “capacity” to resist reasonable ambition and change.

### 7. Derived Principles

The following are proposed downstream rules, not accepted Spark OS Principles:

- Grow through sufficient, not perfect, readiness when risk is bounded and learning is possible.
- Distinguish temporary strain from structural fragility.
- Do not use capability language to protect stagnation.

## Build for Those Who Follow

### 1. Name

Build for Those Who Follow

### 2. Commitment

Spark Mortgages accepts proportionate present cost so foreseeable future employees, leaders, maintainers, successors, and clients materially affected by continuity do not inherit reasonably avoidable ignorance, unexplained systems, repeated consequential mistakes, or unnecessary loss.

### 3. Meaning

Some people affected by today’s decisions cannot presently advocate for themselves. This Value requires Spark to consider what foreseeable future Spark people and continuity-dependent clients should reasonably inherit.

Spark cannot preserve lived experience itself. It can preserve only representations such as evidence, rationale, decisions, uncertainty, dissent, lessons, and context. Those representations are incomplete, and synthesis may lose assumptions, provenance, disagreement, and edge cases.

Preservation is not inherently good. Responsible stewardship includes selective preservation, transmission, challenge, retirement, deletion, and sometimes intentional relearning. Repository presence does not make information true, authoritative, current, canonical, complete, or applicable.

The purpose is to help future people understand and improve what they inherit, not force them to repeat past conclusions. Spark should not preserve everything or promise freedom from relearning. The obligation is proportionate to consequence, foreseeable usefulness, recurrence risk, avoidable reconstruction, and the burden or risk of preservation.

### 4. Observable Behavior

Spark is living this Value when it:

- preserves consequential knowledge and rationale whose loss would create avoidable future harm or relearning;
- transmits critical understanding before foreseeable transitions;
- keeps inherited knowledge open to challenge and replacement;
- and deliberately retires or deletes material that should not continue.

### 5. Trade-off

Spark accepts present work and cost whose main benefit may arise only after current participants leave. It also accepts deletion, retirement, or loss of historical completeness when law, privacy, security, misleading context, maintenance burden, or better future judgment makes continued preservation irresponsible.

### 6. Failure Mode

The central violation is allowing foreseeable future Spark people to inherit preventable ignorance, unexplained systems, or repeated consequential mistakes because continuity offered no immediate benefit.

This Value can be gamed through excessive preservation: retaining everything “just in case,” treating stored information as authoritative, protecting founder preference or obsolete guidance from challenge, or using possible future usefulness to justify surveillance or indefinite retention.

### 7. Derived Principles

The following are proposed downstream rules, not accepted Spark OS Principles:

- Accept proportionate present cost for foreseeable future continuity.
- Preserve reconstructability where loss would be materially consequential.
- Preserve and retire deliberately so future people can understand, challenge, and improve what they inherit.
